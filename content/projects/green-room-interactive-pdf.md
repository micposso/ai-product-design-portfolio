# The Green Room with Paul Provenza

## Summary

Around 2016, during one of my first projects in media and entertainment, I worked on rethinking how press materials were delivered for The Green Room with Paul Provenza on Showtime. The goal was to replace a static, forgettable press kit with something that better reflected the show's rhythm, tone, and personality.

## Context

Traditional press kits at the time followed a predictable formula: long PDFs, static imagery, and a heavy emphasis on information over experience. For a show like The Green Room, built around unscripted, raw conversations between comedians, that format flattened everything distinctive about the program into something generic.

The issue was not that the materials lacked information. The issue was that they failed to communicate what the show actually felt like.

## Challenge

Traditional press kits were long, static, and text-heavy, which made them a poor fit for a show built around unscripted, raw conversations between comedians.

The gap was not informational. It was experiential.

## What I Built

- Reframed the press kit as an interactive artifact instead of a static document.
- Designed a rich interactive PDF to communicate the tone and pacing of the show.
- Embedded audio moments so viewers could hear the rhythm of the conversations directly.
- Added layered navigation to support both skimming and deeper exploration.
- Used transitions and media previews to create a more narrative, guided experience.

## Discovery

The core insight was that the problem was not informational but experiential: the existing press-kit format flattened the show's tone into something generic and easy to ignore.

## Build

The press kit was redesigned as an interactive PDF with embedded audio clips, layered navigation, integrated media previews, and subtle transitions inspired by the show's backstage atmosphere.

## Launch

The final deliverable gave media professionals a more direct sense of the show's pacing and personality, creating a richer path from first impression to editorial understanding.

## Body

The challenge was clear from the beginning. Traditional press kits did not reflect the personality of the show. They were static, predictable, and easy to ignore. For a series built on unscripted conversations, sharp humor, and the energy of comedians interacting in real time, that kind of delivery system simply did not work.

Instead of treating the press kit as a document, the project reframed it as an interactive artifact. The solution was a rich, interactive PDF designed to guide the viewer through the show in a more dynamic way.

Key elements included embedded audio moments that let media professionals immediately understand the rhythm and tone of the conversations. Animated transitions created movement between sections and were loosely inspired by the show's opening sequence of moving into a backstage environment. Layered navigation allowed users to skim quickly or go deeper into areas like guest dynamics, production details, and the structure of the format. Integrated media previews reduced friction by keeping important moments accessible directly inside the document instead of requiring external links.

At the time, this approach was unusual. Most press kits were passive. This one required interaction. It showed instead of told, controlled pacing through design, and created a narrative flow within a format that was usually treated as flat and purely informational.

That difference mattered. The interactive design helped recipients spend more time with the material and develop a clearer understanding of what made the show distinct. For writers and media contacts, that meant more accurate coverage and a stronger editorial grasp of the show's voice and premise.

It also created differentiation. In a crowded television landscape, even the format of the press kit became part of the signal. It suggested that this was not a conventional talk show and that the experience of the series deserved a more immersive introduction.

The key insight from the project was simple:

When the product is dynamic, the communication cannot be static.

Looking back, this project stands out as an early shift in how I approached digital experiences. It was less about designing assets and more about designing how information is experienced over time.

## Outcome

The interactive format increased engagement, gave media contacts a stronger editorial understanding of the show, and helped differentiate it in a crowded television landscape.
